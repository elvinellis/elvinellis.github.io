Communications tips:
Visionaries (INFJ, INTJ)
  • Use models and show the overall framework of ideas
  • Focus on long-term planning and visions
  • Provide complex concepts and theories rather than facts and details
  • Use metaphors, symbols, and other figures of speech
  • Provide opportunities for them to question and seek new information
  • Show ways to change systems or improve processes
  • Relate experiences to theories and mental models
  • Use precise languages and watch out for multiple meanings of language
Assimilators (ISTJ, ISFJ)
  • Be detailed, accurate and precise
  • Focus on facts, real-life examples, and what is known
  • Make sure information is useful and relevant
  • Listen to their expertise and experience
  • Be practical; avoid abstraction, metaphors, and symbolism
  • Link what is new to what exists; integrate information with past experiences and knowledge
  • Provide step-by-step and structured explanations
  • Provide enough information for them to get a comprehensive understanding
  • Avoid surprises and ambiguity; provide detailed information as soon as possible
Intuitives under stress (INTJ, INFJ, ENTP, ENFP)
  • Get obsessed with trivial details even when it's not important
  • Preoccupied with irrelevant facts
  • Over indulge in eating, drinking, exercise, etc.
Sensors under stress (ISTJ, ISFJ, ESTP, ESFP)
  • See the future negatively
  • Gets unduly pessimistic
  • Gets stuck in the rut
  • Don't see possible ways out
A model for managing conflict
Step 1: Create Space
Bringing the views, ideas, and opinions of all vested parties into the dialogue. Fathering facts, verifying assumptions and seeking clarification.
Objective: not only to know diverse views but to understand them.
Action: Change location; active listening, taking breakers, seeking a mediator

Step 2: Add Value
Acknowledge the contribution of one's natural preference for decision making hen augment this with the contribution of the opposite function.

Step 3: Seek Closure
Key activities: (1) Agreeing on Decision Principles, (2) Taking one step at a time, (3) Defining next steps, (4) Closing the book
If you are a TJ
Create space:
  • Resist your tendency to rush to closure/resolution
  • Practice active listening
  • Use your natural ability to define a reasonable process for all parties involved
Add value:
  • Remember the feelings of others are valid inputs and deserve consideration
  • Silence your internal critic for a while
  • Use your logical analysis to contribute to the process
Seek closure:
  • Keep in mind a drive for total victory may kill small gains
  • Remember, other parties may not be ready to move to this stage if their feelings have not been accepted
  • Having heard all sides, help everyone push toward a resolution
If you are a FJ
Create space:
  • Your tendency is to avoid conflict at all costs
  • Your desire for harmony typically makes you uncomfortable standing your ground for very long
  • Use your natural ability to welcome others and their feelings into the dialogue
Add value:
  • Your need for harmony restricts your ability to objectively challenge and critique ideas
  • You may be reluctant to argue a point that challenges others' view but that can add value to the process
  • Your ability to keep all parties engaged and open is extremely valuable
Seek closure:
  • Remember, not everyone views disagreement as irreparably damaging to a relationship
  • Other parties may view your emphasis on feelings as "too soft" or unfocused
  • Your strength in empathizing with all parties and ensuring everyone's needs are met is valued by others
In dealing with TJs, be aware that
Create space:
  • Their fears regarding the uncertainty of the outcome are very strong
  • The value of hearing others' viewpoints might need to be reinforced
  • Succinct and direct input is more likely to be heard
Add value:
  • Starting with their logic basis and broadening out can enable them to encompass feeling
  • Their criticism is not personal
  • Safe space is required for them to share their feelings or be open to other's feeling
Seek closure:
  • Once the conflict is closed, they are not likely to reopen it, so be sure you are ready to move on
  • It is likely that they have not addressed emotional aspect of the conflict; these may need to be reconciled first
  • All parties must be prepared to act on what has been agreed
In dealing with FJs, be aware that
Create space:
  • They tend to see conflict as painful, negative, and personal experience
  • The feelings of everyone are important to them
  • They may try to rush through the process to avoid the discomfort they experience associated with conflict
Add value:
  • Their focus is first to ensure that everyone is okay with the dialogue
  • Their emotionalism and intensity are not embarassing or inappropriate to them
  • Once they are certain everyone's concerns and feelings have been given credence, they can calmly look at other factors
Seek closure:
  • If there is ill will or lingering frustration, they will not be able to close comfortably even if the decision is unanimous
  • Their "extreme interest" in what you are feeling is not intended to frustrate you or negate the importance of the issue
  • They may later feel pushed aside if their views were not invited in before closing
